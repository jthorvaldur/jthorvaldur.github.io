II. Executive Summary

This complaint concerns a pattern of conduct by attorney Annemarie Tarara in representing Heather Atagan (Respondent) in the above-captioned domestic relations case. The pattern includes:

  1. Filing a Financial Affidavit that omits at least 9 accounts and $190,000+ in assets, including accounts counsel demonstrably knew about;
  2. Filing three successive Contempt Petitions seeking body attachment while crediting Petitioner with only $32,641 of $366,929 in documented payments;
  3. Misrepresenting the nature of bank transfers during settlement negotiations, attributing Respondent's own accounts to Petitioner;
  4. Refusing to respond to any discovery requests or meet-and-confer letters over a five-month period; and
  5. Engaging in a broader pattern of conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, and deceit, including facilitating Respondent's active concealment of assets during litigation.

The misconduct is not isolated. It represents a coordinated litigation strategy that weaponizes the contempt process against a party who has demonstrably paid hundreds of thousands of dollars in support, while systematically concealing the very accounts those payments flowed into.

VIII. Legal Precedents & Authorities

Contempt & Civil Procedure Standards
  • In re Marriage of Sharp, 369 Ill. App. 3d 271, 280 (2d Dist. 2006) Contempt must be proven by clear and convincing evidence. The burden rests on the petitioner to establish both the existence of a valid order and the respondent's willful violation of that order.
  • In re Marriage of Logston, 103 Ill. 2d 266, 277 (1984) Purge conditions in civil contempt must be achievable. A respondent cannot be held in contempt for failure to do the impossible; purge conditions must be within the contemnor's power to perform.
  • 750 ILCS 5/501(a)(3) In a proceeding for dissolution of marriage, each party has a continuing duty to disclose to the other party all assets, income, and financial information relevant to the proceeding.
  • Cook County Local Rule 13.3.1 Parties have an ongoing duty to supplement financial disclosures. A Financial Affidavit that becomes inaccurate must be amended and re-filed.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 201(k) Parties shall meet and confer in good faith regarding discovery disputes before seeking court intervention. The meet-and-confer requirement imposes an affirmative obligation to respond.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 137 Every pleading, motion, or other document signed by an attorney certifies that to the best of the attorney's knowledge it is well grounded in fact. Sanctions may be imposed for false or frivolous pleadings.
Attorney Discipline Standards
  • In re Himmel, 125 Ill. 2d 531 (1988) An attorney has an obligation to report knowledge of another attorney's misconduct. Failure to report is itself a disciplinary violation. By extension, an attorney who facilitates client fraud bears direct responsibility.
  • In re Winthrop, 219 Ill. 2d 526 (2006) Attorney discipline is appropriate where an attorney assists a client in misconduct. An attorney who knowingly files false documents or assists in concealment of assets may be subject to discipline up to and including disbarment.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 3.3(a)(1) A lawyer shall not knowingly make a false statement of material fact or law to a tribunal or fail to correct a false statement of material fact or law previously made to the tribunal.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 3.1 A lawyer shall not bring or defend a proceeding, or assert or controvert an issue therein, unless there is a basis in law and fact for doing so that is not frivolous.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 4.1(a) In the course of representing a client, a lawyer shall not knowingly make a false statement of material fact or law to a third person.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 3.4(a), (d) A lawyer shall not unlawfully obstruct another party's access to evidence, and shall not fail to make reasonably diligent effort to comply with a legally proper discovery request.
  • Illinois Supreme Court Rule 8.4(c) It is professional misconduct for a lawyer to engage in conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation.

IX. Conclusion & Requested Relief

The conduct described in this complaint is not a matter of aggressive advocacy or zealous representation. It is a pattern of affirmative dishonesty directed at the tribunal and the opposing party, sustained over more than a year.

Attorney Tarara filed a verified Financial Affidavit she knew or should have known was materially false. She filed three successive Contempt Petitions seeking the arrest of a party who had paid $366,929 — while crediting him with $32,641. She refused to respond to any discovery request over a five-month period. She facilitated her client's active concealment of assets, including the renaming of a Canadian bank account during active litigation.

Her own filing — the Second Contempt Petition's Exhibit C — contains the proof. It references account ...9056 by name, an account omitted from the Financial Affidavit filed five months earlier. This is not inadvertence. It is a pattern.

The Complainant respectfully requests that the ARDC:

  1. Open a formal investigation into attorney Annemarie Tarara, ARDC No. 6284380;
  2. Examine the Financial Affidavit of May 15, 2025 against the documented accounts and transfers;
  3. Review the three Contempt Petitions and their stated payment credits against documented transfers;
  4. Investigate counsel's failure to respond to discovery requests and meet-and-confer obligations;
  5. Determine whether counsel knowingly facilitated the concealment of assets from the tribunal; and
  6. Impose appropriate discipline consistent with the nature and severity of the violations.
