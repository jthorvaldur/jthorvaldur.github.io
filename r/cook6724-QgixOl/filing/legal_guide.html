1. Foundations — How a Divorce Case Works

The Basic Structure

An Illinois divorce (technically a "Dissolution of Marriage") has several phases:

Phase 1: Petition — One party files a Petition for Dissolution. The other is served.
Phase 2: Temporary Orders — Court sets interim rules (who lives where, temporary support, parenting schedule).
Phase 3: Discovery — Both sides exchange financial records, documents, interrogatories.
Phase 4: Negotiation/Mediation — Parties try to settle.
Phase 5: Pre-Trial — If no settlement, prepare for trial. Exchange witness lists, exhibits.
Phase 6: Trial — Judge hears evidence and decides everything the parties couldn't agree on.
Phase 7: Judgment — Final order dissolving the marriage, dividing property, setting support.

Key Statute

750 ILCS 5/ — The Illinois Marriage and Dissolution of Marriage Act (IMDMA). This is the master statute. Everything in your case flows from it.

Petitioner vs. Respondent

The person who filed is the Petitioner (you). The other party is the Respondent (Heather). These labels don't mean plaintiff/defendant — there's no "suing." Both parties have equal rights and obligations.

2. Discovery — Getting Information

Discovery is the formal process of getting information from the other side. This is where most divorce cases are won or lost — not at trial. If you can prove what the other side has (or is hiding), the judge's decisions follow naturally.

Types of Discovery

Interrogatories you can send
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 213
Written questions the other side must answer under oath within 28 days. Example: "List all bank accounts you have held since January 1, 2020, including the institution name, account number, and current balance."

Limit: 30 interrogatories in Cook County without leave of court.
Requests to Produce you can send
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 214
Demand that the other side hand over specific documents. Example: "Produce all bank statements for Chase account ending in ...9056 from November 2024 to present."

Response due: 28 days. They must produce the documents or state specific objections.
Requests to Admit either side
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 216
Ask the other side to admit or deny specific facts. If they don't respond in 28 days, the facts are deemed admitted. Very powerful.

Example: "Admit that you received 13 transfers totaling $110,041.00 from the joint Chase account ending in ...2151 between November 2024 and February 2026."

If she doesn't respond → it's admitted as fact. The judge treats it as true.
Depositions either side
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 202-206
Live, in-person (or video) questioning under oath, recorded by a court reporter. Expensive but powerful — the witness can't prepare written answers or hide behind their lawyer's objections the same way. Used more in complex cases.
Subpoenas to Third Parties you can send
735 ILCS 5/2-402
When the other side won't produce records, you go directly to the source — the bank, employer, brokerage, crypto exchange. A subpoena duces tecum orders a third party to produce documents.

Key point: You don't need the other side's permission. You do need to give them notice (serve a copy of the subpoena on opposing counsel).
Example: Subpoena Chase Bank for all account statements for accounts ...9056 and ...2573.
Example: Subpoena Fidelity for 401(k) distribution records and Form 1099-R.
Example: Subpoena Gemini for cryptocurrency transaction history.

This is how you get records when the other side stonewalls. The bank doesn't care about the divorce — they just comply with valid subpoenas.

The 28-Day Rule

Most discovery responses are due within 28 days of service (not filing). If they don't respond, you file a Motion to Compel. If they still don't respond after a court order, you seek sanctions.

Meet and Confer Requirement

Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 201(k) Before filing any discovery motion, you must make a good-faith attempt to resolve the dispute with the other side. In practice: send an email saying "please produce X, or I'll file a motion." Keep a copy. This is what the meet-and-confer email does.

3. Motions — Asking the Court to Do Something

A motion is a formal written request asking the judge to take a specific action. Every motion has the same structure:

1. Caption — case name, number, judge
2. Title — what you're asking for
3. "NOW COMES..." — opening identifying who you are
4. Numbered paragraphs — facts supporting your request
5. "WHEREFORE..." (Prayer for Relief) — the specific orders you want
6. Signature — /s/ Your Name
7. Notice of Motion — separate one-page document telling the other side when/where you'll present it

Common Motions in Your Case

Motion to Compel offensive
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 219(a)
"Judge, I asked for documents. They didn't produce them. Please order them to." This is the workhorse of discovery enforcement. The court orders the other side to produce within a set time (usually 14-21 days).

Prerequisites: (1) You made a discovery request; (2) they didn't respond or responded inadequately; (3) you attempted meet-and-confer.

In your case: Heather's financial affidavit is 11 months old, missing $270K+ in assets. You've asked twice (Dec 2025). No response. Meet-and-confer sent April 17. Motion to Compel is ready to file.
Motion in Limine defensive
Common law / IRE 403
"Judge, before the hearing starts, please rule that certain evidence is excluded." Filed before a hearing or trial. It sets the ground rules for what the other side can talk about.

Why it matters: If Heather hasn't disclosed her finances honestly, she shouldn't be able to use financial arguments (like "Joel owes X in arrears") that are based on her incomplete numbers. The motion says: "You can't use evidence you refused to produce properly."

"In limine" is Latin for "at the threshold" — you're asking the judge to decide the issue before the hearing even begins.
Motion to Continue procedural
Asks to postpone a hearing date. Common reasons: need more time to prepare, waiting for discovery, scheduling conflict, party is out of state/country. Courts grant these routinely if you have a good reason and ask early enough. Usually combined with a proposed new date.
Motion for Remote Appearance procedural
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 45
Asks permission to appear by video (Zoom) instead of in person. Routinely granted when a party lives far away. You're in Canada — this is a reasonable and common request.
Motion for Sanctions offensive
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 219(c) Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 137
"Judge, they disobeyed your order. Please impose consequences." Filed after the other side ignores a court order (like an order to compel). Sanctions can include:

- Adverse inference: judge assumes the hidden evidence is bad for the hiding party
- Barring evidence: they can't use what they didn't disclose
- Striking pleadings: their contempt petitions get thrown out
- Attorney fees: they pay your costs (or paralegal costs)
- Contempt: in extreme cases, jail
Emergency Motion either side
Cook County Local Rule 13.2
For urgent situations that can't wait for a regular hearing date. Must show immediate, irreparable harm. Example: Heather filed emergency motions for a TRO and an order of protection against you in April 2025. The bar is supposed to be high, but domestic relations courts grant them more freely.
Petition to Modify offensive
750 ILCS 5/510
Asks the court to change an existing order (support amount, parenting time, etc.) based on a "substantial change in circumstances." Your $0 income vs. the income assumed when the maintenance order was entered is a textbook changed circumstance.

4. Contempt — When Someone Disobeys a Court Order

Contempt is serious — it's one of the few areas in civil law where you can go to jail. Understanding it is critical because you're facing two contempt petitions.

Two Types of Civil Contempt

Direct Contempt
Something that happens in front of the judge (yelling in court, refusing to answer a question on the stand). Rare. Not your situation.
Indirect Civil Contempt what you're facing
750 ILCS 5/508
Disobeying a court order outside the courtroom. In your case: allegedly not paying maintenance as ordered.

The petitioner (Heather) must prove:
1. A valid court order existed
2. You knew about it
3. You violated it

Your defense (the "purge"):
Once they prove those three things, the burden shifts to you to show you couldn't comply — not that you didn't want to, but that you literally could not. This is called the inability defense. Your $0 income and the $367K you've already paid are your evidence here.

The purge plan: The court may set a "purge" — a specific amount you must pay to avoid jail. The amount must be something you're actually able to pay. A purge you can't afford is unconstitutional.

Key case: In re Marriage of Sharp, 369 Ill. App. 3d 271 (2006) — the petitioner must prove the violation by clear and convincing evidence, not just a preponderance. This is a higher bar.

Body Attachment

A body attachment is an arrest warrant. If the court finds you in contempt and you don't purge, the court can issue a body attachment — meaning if you set foot in Illinois, you can be arrested and brought to court. This is the nuclear option and courts use it reluctantly. Your Motion to Continue / Remote Appearance and your $367K payment record are your shields here.

Rule to Show Cause

An order requiring you to appear and explain why you shouldn't be held in contempt. The Feb 20, 2026 order in your case included a Rule to Show Cause. This is the formal mechanism that creates the May 5 hearing.

5. Evidence Rules — What Gets In, What Stays Out

Relevance

IRE 401-402 Evidence must be relevant — it must make a fact "more or less probable." In a divorce, almost all financial information is relevant. The judge has wide latitude.

Hearsay

IRE 801-807 An out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of what it asserts. Generally not admissible, with many exceptions:

Party Admission (IRE 801(d)(2)): Anything the other side said or wrote can be used against them. Heather's email to her accountant ("120-150K from Fidelity and 40K crypto from Gemini") is a party admission — fully admissible, no hearsay problem.

Business Records (IRE 803(6)): Bank statements, transaction records, account statements — all admissible as business records. The bank's custodian of records certifies them.

Public Records (IRE 803(8)): Court filings, tax returns, government records — admissible.

Documents Affecting Property Interest (IRE 803(15)): Deeds, titles, account registrations.

Authentication

IRE 901 You must prove a document is what you say it is. For emails: show the email headers (from, to, date, subject). For bank records: get them from the bank with a certification letter. For your own records: testify "I sent this email on [date]."

Adverse Inference

When a party destroys evidence or refuses to produce it, the court may instruct that the missing evidence would have been unfavorable to that party. This is powerful — if Heather won't produce her Fidelity 1099-R, the judge can assume it shows something she's trying to hide.

6. Financial Disclosure — The Core of Every Divorce

The Duty to Disclose

750 ILCS 5/501(a)(3) 750 ILCS 5/503
Both parties have an absolute duty to disclose all assets, all income, all debts. This is not optional. This is not "produce what you feel like." The statute says "all assets... and all income from whatever source derived."

Financial Affidavit

750 ILCS 5/504 Cook County Local Rule 13.3.1
A sworn, verified statement of your income, expenses, assets, and liabilities. Filed under oath. If it's false, it's perjury. Both sides must file one. Heather's is materially incomplete — she omitted $270K+ in known assets.

What "Complete" Disclosure Looks Like

CategoryWhat must be disclosed
Bank accountsEvery account — checking, savings, money market, CDs — at every institution, in every country
Investment accountsBrokerage, mutual funds, stocks, bonds
Retirement accounts401(k), IRA, pension, annuity — and any distributions taken
CryptocurrencyEvery exchange account, wallet, and holding
Business interestsAny business owned or operated, including revenue, expenses, and accounts
Real propertyHouses, land, rental property — value, mortgage, equity
VehiclesCars, boats, etc.
IncomeAll sources — employment, business, investment, rental, gifts, inheritance
DebtsCredit cards, loans, mortgages, student loans, tax obligations
InsuranceLife, health, property — policies and beneficiaries

Dissipation

750 ILCS 5/503(d)(2)
When one spouse uses marital assets for a non-marital purpose after the marriage has broken down. If Heather took a $150K 401(k) distribution during the case and spent it on non-marital expenses, that's potential dissipation. The burden shifts to the dissipating party to prove the money was spent on legitimate marital purposes.

7. Parental Responsibilities (Custody/Visitation)

Illinois doesn't use the words "custody" and "visitation" anymore. Since 2016, the statute uses:

Allocation of Parental Responsibilities = who makes major decisions (education, health, religion, extracurriculars)

Parenting Time = the schedule of when each parent has the children

Parenting Plan = the comprehensive document covering both, plus communication protocols, transportation, holidays, etc.

Best Interest Standard

750 ILCS 5/602.7
All parenting decisions are based on the "best interest of the child." The statute lists 17 factors the judge considers, including each parent's willingness to facilitate a relationship with the other parent, the child's wishes, and any history of abuse or domestic violence.

Parenting Conduct Orders

Court orders restricting what parents can do — like not disparaging the other parent, not discussing the case with children, not introducing children to new partners. Violation can be contempt.

Our Family Wizard (OFW)

A court-ordered communication platform. Your docket shows an OFW order from April 28, 2025. All parenting communication is supposed to go through OFW — not text, not email. Non-use can be raised as a contempt issue (though it goes both ways).

8. Maintenance (Alimony) and Child Support

Maintenance (Alimony)

750 ILCS 5/504

Temporary Maintenance: Set during the case, before final judgment. Based on a statutory formula or judge's discretion. Can be modified if circumstances change.

Guideline Formula: 33.33% of payor's net income minus 25% of payee's net income. But the total can't exceed 40% of combined net income.

Duration: Based on length of marriage. For marriages of 20+ years, court can order permanent maintenance.

Modification: Either party can petition to modify if there's a "substantial change in circumstances." Going from employed to $0 income qualifies.

Child Support

750 ILCS 5/505
Based on the "income shares" model — both parents' incomes are combined, then allocated proportionally. The number of overnights each parent has affects the calculation. Illinois uses standardized guidelines.

Arrears

Unpaid support that has accumulated. Arrears are a judgment — they don't go away, they accrue interest, and they can be enforced through contempt, wage garnishment, tax intercepts, and passport denial. However, payments/transfers that aren't formally credited can be argued as offsets.

9. Property Division

Marital vs. Non-Marital Property

750 ILCS 5/503

Marital property: Almost everything acquired during the marriage, regardless of whose name it's in. Divided "equitably" (fairly, not necessarily 50/50).

Non-marital property: Property owned before the marriage, gifts to one spouse, inheritance. Generally stays with the owning spouse — but can be "transmuted" into marital property if commingled.

Key point: The marital home is marital property even if only one name is on the deed or mortgage. Both spouses have an interest.

Equitable Distribution Factors

The judge considers 12 statutory factors including: length of marriage, each party's contribution, each party's economic circumstances, any dissipation, any prenuptial agreement, and the needs of the children.

10. Court Procedures — How Hearings Actually Work

Types of Court Dates

TypeWhat happens
Status CallQuick check-in. Judge asks "where are we?" Both sides report. Takes 2-5 minutes. No testimony.
Motion CallJudge hears and rules on pending motions. You present your argument (briefly), the other side responds, judge decides. Usually 5-15 minutes per motion.
Pre-Trial ConferenceJudge and both sides discuss settlement possibilities. Judge may express preliminary views to push settlement. Not binding.
Prove-UpIf you've reached an agreement, you present it to the judge for approval. Brief testimony confirming the terms.
Evidentiary HearingMini-trial on a specific issue. Witnesses testify, exhibits are entered, cross-examination happens. This is what a contempt hearing looks like.
TrialFull presentation of all disputed issues. Can take hours or days. Judge issues a written judgment afterward.

Presenting a Motion

When your motion is called:

1. Stand when the judge addresses you
2. "Good morning/afternoon, Your Honor. Joel Thorarinson, pro se petitioner."
3. State what you're asking for in 1-2 sentences
4. Give your best 2-3 supporting facts
5. Stop talking. Let the other side respond.
6. If the judge asks questions, answer directly — don't ramble
7. "Thank you, Your Honor."

Golden rule: Be brief. Judges handle 20-40 cases per call. They've read your papers. Don't re-read your motion out loud.

Objections

During evidentiary hearings, you can object to improper questions or evidence. Common objections: hearsay, relevance, foundation (hasn't been authenticated), leading (on direct examination), asked and answered. If you don't know whether to object, let it go — the judge will weigh the evidence regardless.

11. Sanctions — Consequences for Bad Behavior

Rule 137 Sanctions
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 137
For filing documents that are not "well grounded in fact." A financial affidavit that omits $270K is arguably a Rule 137 violation. Sanctions can include attorney fees and costs.
Rule 219(c) Sanctions
Ill. Sup. Ct. R. 219(c)
For discovery violations. The escalation ladder:
1. Order to comply (Motion to Compel)
2. Adverse inference
3. Bar evidence/witnesses
4. Strike pleadings
5. Default judgment
6. Contempt
7. Attorney fees at every level

Courts prefer to go up the ladder, not skip to the top. This is why the sequence matters: ask → meet and confer → motion to compel → sanctions. Each step shows the judge you tried to resolve it.
Section 508(b) Fees
750 ILCS 5/508(b)
The court can order either party to pay the other's attorney fees (or costs) if one party's failure to comply with discovery caused the other to incur unnecessary expense. Even as a pro se litigant, you can recover filing fees and paralegal costs.

12. Pro Se Practice — Representing Yourself

Your Rights

You have the constitutional right to represent yourself. The judge will hold you to the same procedural rules as a lawyer, but most judges give pro se litigants some leeway on formatting and procedure — especially in domestic relations court. They will not, however, give you substantive legal advice from the bench.

Practical Tips

File everything. If it's not in the court file, it didn't happen. Conversations, emails, agreements — put them on the record.

Be organized. Judges notice. Numbered exhibits, clear timelines, concise filings. The judge has 30 other cases that day.

Be respectful. Always "Your Honor." Never argue with the judge. If you disagree with a ruling, note your objection and move on.

Don't be emotional. The facts and the law win cases. "She hid $270,000 in assets" is more powerful than "she's lying and dishonest."

Keep a paper trail. Every email, every request, every response (or non-response). Date and save everything.

Use a paralegal. You don't need a lawyer for everything. A paralegal can handle formatting, filing, service, and procedural guidance at a fraction of the cost.

The Appearance Form

ATJ 111.5
This is the form you filed today. It tells the court: "I'm participating in this case and here's how to reach me." It must be filed when you transition from represented to pro se. Without it, the court may not send you notices or recognize your filings.

Responding to Opposing Counsel's Requests

Tarara asked for your file-stamped appearance. This is standard — she needs it for her records and to confirm who to serve. You can send it once you receive the file-stamped copy back from the court (eFileIL will email it to you). No rush, no games — just a procedural exchange.
