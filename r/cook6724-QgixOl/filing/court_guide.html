Appellate Court Key Facts

  • No new evidence — review is on the trial court record only
  • Panel of 3 justices (sometimes en banc for significant questions)
  • Oral argument is optional and time-limited (usually 15 min per side)
  • Decision by written opinion, not from the bench
  • From the Appellate Court, further appeal goes to the Illinois Supreme Court (discretionary, via Petition for Leave to Appeal)

How Appeals Work from Your Case

  • Final orders from Judge Jannusch can be appealed to the 1st District Appellate Court
  • You have 30 days from entry of the order to file a Notice of Appeal (Supreme Court Rule 303)
  • Interlocutory (non-final) orders generally require a Petition for Leave to Appeal under Rule 306
  • The Appellate Court reviews the trial court record — no new evidence, no new witnesses
  • Standard of review varies: abuse of discretion (most family law), de novo (questions of law), manifest weight of the evidence (factual findings)

Title IV-D Explained — Federal Child Support Enforcement

Title IV-D refers to Title IV, Part D of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. 651-669b), which established the federal child support enforcement program. Understanding this system is important because it operates parallel to and sometimes intersects with private dissolution cases like yours.

What Is Title IV-D?

  • Federal mandate: Every state must operate a child support enforcement program to receive federal funding for public assistance
  • Purpose: Ensure children receive financial support from non-custodial parents, reducing reliance on public aid
  • Scope: Locating parents, establishing paternity, establishing support orders, modifying orders, enforcing orders, collecting payments
  • Universal availability: Any parent (not just public assistance recipients) can apply for IV-D services for a nominal fee ($25)

Regular Family Court (Your Case)

  • Private action between spouses
  • Parties hire own attorneys (or pro se)
  • Covers everything: property, maintenance, custody, support
  • Judge has broad discretion
  • Discovery process is party-driven
  • Enforcement via contempt motions (party files)
  • Case No. format: 2024 D 6724

Title IV-D Court

  • State is a party (SA or AG represents the state's interest)
  • State provides the attorney — parents may also have counsel
  • Only child support (not property, maintenance, or custody)
  • Administrative tools: wage garnishment, license suspension, tax intercept
  • State handles investigation and enforcement
  • High-volume, streamlined calendar
  • Can cross state lines via UIFSA (Uniform Interstate Family Support Act)

When Cases Get Referred to IV-D

  • Public assistance: Automatic referral if custodial parent receives TANF, Medicaid, or SNAP benefits
  • Application: Either parent can apply for IV-D services independently
  • Interstate: When the obligor lives in a different state (UIFSA proceedings)
  • Non-payment: Private cases may be referred for enforcement when regular contempt proceedings fail

How IV-D Intersects with Private Dissolution

  • A IV-D case can run parallel to your private dissolution — different courtrooms, different judges, different case numbers
  • IV-D orders and private court orders must be reconciled — conflicting support orders create enforcement problems
  • The IV-D agency has enforcement powers your private case does not:
    • Automatic income withholding (wage garnishment)
    • Federal/state tax refund intercept
    • Passport denial (for arrearages over $2,500)
    • Driver's license / professional license suspension
    • Credit bureau reporting
    • Federal criminal prosecution (Deadbeat Parents Punishment Act, 18 U.S.C. 228)
  • If your private case results in a child support order, the State Disbursement Unit (SDU) typically processes payments regardless of IV-D status

Illinois-Specific: Division of Child Support Services (DCSS)

  • Operated by the Illinois Department of Healthcare and Family Services (HFS)
  • Cook County IV-D cases are heard in the Daley Center but in designated courtrooms separate from Domestic Relations Division
  • The Cook County State's Attorney represents the state in IV-D proceedings (not the AG, in Cook County specifically)
  • IV-D case workers handle case management, locate absent parents, calculate guidelines support
  • Illinois uses income shares model for child support calculation (750 ILCS 5/505)
