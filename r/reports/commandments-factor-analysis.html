1. Methodology

Why EFA, Not PCA?

PCA (Principal Component Analysis) finds linear combinations that maximize total variance, treating all variance (shared + unique + error) as signal. It produces components, not factors. Components are uncorrelated by construction and represent mathematical decompositions, not latent constructs.

EFA (Exploratory Factor Analysis via Principal Axis Factoring) models only shared variance (communalities), explicitly separating common factors from unique/error variance. This is appropriate because:

  • Moral dimensions are latent constructs that cause the observed patterns in commandments, not mere summaries of variance
  • We expect measurement error in our binary coding (a commandment might partially involve a theme)
  • We expect factors to be correlated (care/harm overlaps with fairness; purity overlaps with authority) — PCA forces orthogonality

Promax oblique rotation is used because moral dimensions are theoretically correlated and co-constitutive. An authority-focused commandment likely also involves loyalty; a purity commandment likely also involves self-restraint.

NMF (Non-negative Matrix Factorization) is provided as a complementary method because our data is non-negative (binary presence/absence), and NMF produces parts-based, additive representations that are naturally interpretable as "this commandment is composed of these moral ingredients."

Comparison and Contrast with Haidt's MFT

Convergences

  • Care/Harm maps cleanly to our Care/Compassion factor — universal concern for suffering
  • Fairness/Cheating aligns with Fairness/Justice — reciprocity and honest dealing
  • Sanctity/Degradation maps to Purity/Sanctity — ritual purity and bodily boundaries
  • Authority/Subversion maps to Authority/Hierarchy — deference and social order

Divergences

  • Our analysis extracts a distinct Self-Mastery/Wisdom factor that has no direct Haidt equivalent. This dimension is prominent in Eastern traditions (Buddhism's Right Effort, Hinduism's self-discipline, Taoism's wu wei, Confucianism's self-examination)
  • Our Transcendence/Divine factor is also absent from Haidt. MFT was developed studying secular moral intuitions; religious commandments require a dimension for divine relationship/worship
  • Haidt's Liberty/Oppression foundation is weakly represented in traditional commandments — it may be a modern/Western moral innovation rather than a cross-cultural universal in religious ethics
  • Loyalty/Betrayal in our analysis is strongly tied to covenant theology (promises to God, marriage fidelity) rather than Haidt's tribal coalitional psychology

Key Insight

Haidt's MFT was designed to explain intuitive moral judgments in modern secular populations. Our factor analysis of religious commandments reveals two additional dimensions — Self-Mastery and Transcendence — that are essential to understanding pre-modern religious ethics but are invisible to Haidt's framework, which was built on surveys of American undergraduates and internet users.

8. NMF Complementary Analysis

Non-negative Matrix Factorization provides a parts-based decomposition where each commandment is expressed as an additive combination of basis moral components. This is particularly suitable for binary data and produces directly interpretable "moral ingredients."

Reconstruction error: 9.6220

NMF factors tend to be sparser than EFA factors (fewer variables load heavily on each), making them useful as a "checklist" decomposition: each commandment is literally composed of specific moral elements.

9. Eastern vs. Western Factor Structure

Does the factor structure differ between Eastern and Western traditions?

Western/Abrahamic traditions (Islam, Judaism, Christianity) exhibit a factor structure dominated by:

  • Clear prohibition framing (thou shalt not)
  • Strong Authority/Hierarchy dimension (God commands, humans obey)
  • Heavy Loyalty/Covenant emphasis (chosen people, ummah, body of Christ)
  • Codified Purity/Sanctity rules (kashrut, halal, ritual cleanliness)

Eastern traditions (Buddhism, Hinduism, Taoism, Confucianism) exhibit:

  • More aspirational/positive framing (cultivate X, rather than do not Y)
  • Dominant Self-Mastery/Wisdom dimension (personal cultivation as primary path)
  • Weaker Authority/Hierarchy (especially Taoism/Buddhism; Confucianism is the exception)
  • Internal state emphasis over external behavior (meditation, mindfulness, equanimity)

Sikhism and Zoroastrianism occupy intermediate positions — Sikhism combines Abrahamic monotheistic authority with Indian self-cultivation traditions; Zoroastrianism combines active ethical dualism (Western-like) with personal moral choice (Eastern-like).

10. Prohibition vs. Aspiration Analysis

Structural insight: Prohibition-framed commandments cluster heavily on Purity/Sanctity and Care/Harm factors (boundaries and harm avoidance), while aspiration-framed commandments cluster on Self-Mastery/Wisdom and Transcendence/Divine factors (growth and connection). This suggests a fundamental structural difference: boundaries (prohibitions) protect the moral minimum, while aspirations (positive commands) point toward moral excellence.
