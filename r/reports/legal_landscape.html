Section IThe Data Flow

Every court filing in the United States begins the same way: a lawyer, or a person representing themselves, creates a document. That document is a public record from the moment it is accepted by the clerk. What happens next is a story of privatization, markup, and artificial scarcity applied to information that belongs to the public.

The filing itself is a public record. The First Amendment guarantees the right to access it. Yet between creation and consumption, at least four toll booths extract rent from information the public already owns.

Step by Step

Step 1: Filing. An attorney creates a motion, complaint, or response. In most jurisdictions, this must now be filed electronically. The filing fee ranges from nothing (fee waivers for indigent filers) to $400+ for initiating civil cases in state courts. The document becomes a public record upon acceptance by the clerk.

Step 2: Ingestion by a Private Platform. The court's Case Management System (CMS) — overwhelmingly Tyler Technologies' Odyssey platform — ingests the filing. Tyler now holds the authoritative digital copy of a public record. The court paid Tyler for this privilege. In many jurisdictions, the public must now pay Tyler (or a Tyler-authorized portal) to access what they already own.

Step 3: Data Licensing. The large legal information companies — Thomson Reuters, RELX (LexisNexis), and Bloomberg — negotiate bulk data licenses with courts, clerks, or intermediaries like Tyler. They add headnotes, citations, classification, and search. The raw $0.10/page public record is now buried inside a $170–$455/month subscription.

Step 4: Analytics Layer. Companies like Lex Machina (owned by LexisNexis), Trellis, and Gavelytics (now Pre/Dicta) apply machine learning to predict judge behavior, estimate case outcomes, and identify litigation patterns. A filing that cost $0 to create now generates intelligence that sells for $5,000–$15,000 per year.

Step 5: Billing. The attorney who accesses this intelligence bills their client $500–$1,200 per hour. A five-minute search that cost the firm $2 in platform fees becomes a $100 line item. The total economic extraction from a single public filing — from creation to client invoice — can exceed 10,000x the underlying cost.

10,000x The potential markup from a public court filing ($0) to the client bill for accessing analytics derived from that filing.

Section IIThe Gatekeepers

Four companies control the flow of legal information in the United States. Their market positions range from near-monopoly to oligopoly, and their combined revenue from legal information exceeds $15 billion annually.

Tyler Technologies

Near-Monopoly NYSE: TYL • ~$2.1B revenue (2025) • Market cap ~$22B

Tyler's Odyssey platform is the case management system for approximately 2,400 courts across 49 states. When a document is e-filed in most US courts, it goes into Tyler's system. Tyler is not merely a vendor — it is the infrastructure layer.

  • Courts & Justice segment: ~$750M revenue
  • Odyssey is the de facto standard CMS
  • Also owns e-filing portals and public access portals
  • Texas SB41 capped public access at $1/document
  • Switching costs for courts are enormous — multi-year, multi-million dollar migrations

Tyler's position is closest to a natural monopoly: courts cannot easily switch, and Tyler controls the data pipeline from filing to public access.

Thomson Reuters (Westlaw)

Oligopoly NYSE: TRI • Legal segment ~$4B revenue • Market cap ~$80B

Thomson Reuters' legal division, anchored by Westlaw, has been the dominant legal research platform since it absorbed West Publishing's century-old citation system. West's Key Number System and headnotes represent a proprietary layer on top of public judicial opinions.

  • Westlaw Edge / Westlaw Precision: $170–$455/month per user
  • Litigation Analytics (fka Westlaw Edge): judge/attorney analytics
  • Practical Law: practice guides and templates
  • CoCounsel: AI assistant powered by GPT-4/Claude
  • Court Express: physical document retrieval service

Thomson Reuters' moat is the classification layer. Raw case law is free (via CourtListener or Google Scholar). Westlaw's value is in the editorial enhancement — headnotes, key numbers, citator signals — that make the free law usable at scale.

RELX / LexisNexis

Oligopoly LSE: REL • Legal segment ~$3.5B revenue • Market cap ~$85B

RELX's legal division owns LexisNexis (legal research), Lex Machina (litigation analytics), and a sprawling data empire that includes public records, news, and risk intelligence. LexisNexis was the first computer-assisted legal research service (1973).

  • Lexis+ AI: $170–$270/month per user
  • Lex Machina: $5,000–$15,000/year — litigation analytics covering 1M+ cases
  • CourtLink: docket search and monitoring
  • Encompasses vast public records databases
  • Also owns risk/compliance tools (Accurint)

Lex Machina represents the apex of legal data monetization: public filings, processed through proprietary NLP, sold back to lawyers at 5–6 figure annual subscriptions for the privilege of understanding patterns in their own court system.

Bloomberg Law

Oligopoly Private (Bloomberg LP) • Legal segment est. ~$1.5B revenue

Bloomberg Law entered the legal research market in 2009, positioning itself as a modern alternative to Westlaw and Lexis. Its key differentiator is integration with Bloomberg's financial data terminal ecosystem.

  • Bloomberg Law: enterprise pricing, typically $500–$800/month per seat
  • Docket search across federal and state courts
  • Litigation Analytics & Points of Law
  • Bloomberg Law AI: brief analysis and drafting assistance
  • Strong in transactional, regulatory, and IP practice areas

Bloomberg's strategy is bundling: law firms that already have Bloomberg terminals face low incremental cost to add Bloomberg Law, creating a loyalty loop that is difficult for competitors to break.

Section IIIThe Constitutional Right

The right of public access to court records is not a policy preference. It is a constitutional principle rooted in the First Amendment, the common law, and the structural requirements of democratic governance. The Supreme Court has affirmed this right repeatedly.

The Common Law Right

The common law right of access to judicial records predates the Constitution. It was recognized in England by the 17th century and adopted wholesale into American jurisprudence. The principle is simple: courts are public institutions that exercise public power, and the public has a right to inspect how that power is exercised.

"It is clear that the courts of this country recognize a general right to inspect and copy public records and documents, including judicial records and documents." — Nixon v. Warner Communications, Inc., 435 U.S. 589, 597 (1978)

In Nixon, the Supreme Court acknowledged this right while holding that it could be limited in specific circumstances (there, the risk of prejudicing pending criminal proceedings). But the default is access. The burden falls on whoever seeks to restrict it.

The First Amendment Right

The Supreme Court recognized a First Amendment right of access to criminal trials in Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia, 448 U.S. 555 (1980). Chief Justice Burger's opinion grounded this right in both history and the structural function of open courts:

"The explicit, guaranteed rights to speak and to publish concerning what takes place at a trial would lose much meaning if access to observe the trial could, as combating combative jurisdictions combated, be foreclosed arbitrarily." — Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia, 448 U.S. 555, 576–77 (1980)

The Court extended this right in the Press-Enterprise cases, establishing a two-part test for when the First Amendment right of access applies:

  1. Experience: Has the proceeding or record historically been open to the public?
  2. Logic: Does public access play a significant positive role in the functioning of the process?

Under this test, virtually all court filings satisfy both prongs. Civil and criminal court proceedings have been open for centuries. Public scrutiny improves the quality of justice, deters corruption, and maintains public confidence in the judiciary.

Key Cases

Case Year Holding Significance
Nixon v. Warner Communications 1978 Common law right to inspect judicial records Established the baseline: public access is the default; restrictions require justification
Richmond Newspapers v. Virginia 1980 First Amendment guarantees right to attend criminal trials Constitutional floor for courtroom access; extended in later cases to other proceedings
Press-Enterprise I 1984 Right of access extends to voir dire Broadened Richmond Newspapers beyond the trial itself
Press-Enterprise II 1986 Two-part "experience and logic" test for access Created the framework courts still use to determine if access rights apply
Hartford Courant v. Pellegrino 2004 First Amendment right extends to docket sheets Critical for court records access — you cannot hide that a case exists

State Constitutional Provisions

Many state constitutions go further than the federal floor. Florida's Article I, Section 24 (the "Sunshine Amendment") provides the broadest access right in the nation: all state and local government records are presumptively public. California, Texas, New York, and most other states have open records statutes that explicitly cover court records.

The Paradox

The constitutional right of access is well-established in law and virtually nonexistent in practice. A right you cannot afford to exercise is not a right at all. When accessing a single state court case requires navigating Tyler Technologies' portal, paying per-document fees, and piecing together information that exists nowhere else in usable form — the right of access has been functionally privatized.

The courts have not addressed whether cost barriers to electronic records constitute a de facto denial of the right of access. This is the central unresolved constitutional question in legal information policy. When PACER charges $0.10 per page for federal records, or Tyler charges $1 per document for state records, are these constitutionally permissible fees — or unconstitutional access barriers?

In National Veterans Legal Services Program v. United States (2020), the Federal Circuit held that PACER fees must be limited to costs necessary to provide public access, not to fund unrelated court technology projects. The implication: fees that exceed the marginal cost of access may violate the statutory framework and, by extension, the constitutional right.

Section VThe Information Asymmetry

The cost structure described above does not affect all participants equally. It creates a systematic advantage for repeat-player litigants over one-time participants, for corporations over individuals, and for the wealthy over everyone else.

Debt Collection: The Machine

The debt collection litigation industry is a high-volume, low-cost operation for plaintiffs and an unaffordable nightmare for defendants. The numbers tell the story:

The Collection Mill

  • Files 500–2,000 cases per month per attorney
  • Uses templates — cost per filing: ~$25 in attorney time
  • Has bulk access to court CMS via Tyler API or equivalent
  • Automated docket monitoring for default deadlines
  • Wins ~80% by default (defendant never responds)
  • Average recovery on a default judgment: ~40% of claim
Cost per case: $25–$150

The Defendant

  • Served once, often by posting (may never see it)
  • No access to bulk court data or analytics
  • Cannot easily determine if the debt is valid, time-barred, or already paid
  • Attorney consultation costs more than many claims
  • Must respond within 20–30 days or lose by default
  • Default judgment leads to wage garnishment, bank levies, credit destruction
Cost to defend: $5,000–$11,500
4 million+ Estimated debt collection lawsuits filed annually in the United States. Approximately 3.2 million end in default judgment. Sources: Pew Charitable Trusts (2020), NCLC estimates, ABA studies

The Specific Harm

Consider a real scenario. A person receives a claim for $9,350 from a debt buyer who purchased the account for $0.04 on the dollar ($374). The filing cost the plaintiff $200. An attorney spent 15 minutes on the template complaint.

The defendant has 20 days to respond. To respond effectively, they need to:

  1. Access the filing to see what was actually alleged
  2. Determine whether the statute of limitations has run
  3. Check whether the debt buyer has standing (proper chain of assignment)
  4. Verify the amount claimed matches any actual debt
  5. Draft an answer with affirmative defenses
  6. File the answer with the court

Steps 1 through 4 require access to legal information. Step 5 requires legal knowledge. Step 6 requires navigating the e-filing system. Each step has a cost barrier. If the defendant fails at any step, they lose by default, and the plaintiff obtains a judgment for the full $9,350 plus attorney fees and costs — often $15,000 or more total.

Who Gets Hurt

Population Size How the System Fails Them
Pro se litigants ~30% of civil cases Cannot access opposing counsel's filings without paying per-document fees. Cannot research applicable law without $170+/month subscriptions. Filing systems are designed for attorneys.
Debt collection defendants 4M+/year 80% default. Of those who respond, many lack information to raise valid defenses. Collection mills have bulk data access; defendants do not.

The information asymmetry in American courts is not a bug. It is the business model. Every actor in the legal information supply chain profits from the fact that one side has information and the other does not. The cost structure ensures that the people who most need access to legal information are the least able to afford it.

Section VIThe Technology

The technology required to solve this problem already exists. There is no fundamental technical barrier to providing free, real-time access to state court records with AI-powered comprehension. The barriers are economic and political, not engineering.

What Courts Actually Run

Tyler Technologies' Odyssey is the dominant case management system. Approximately 2,400 courts across 49 states use it. This is actually an advantage for anyone building a court records access system: one integration covers vast ground.

The Odyssey platform exposes court data through:

  • Public web portals (e.g., re:SearchTX in Texas, Odyssey portals in other states)
  • Tyler's e-filing API (used by attorneys and e-filing service providers)
  • OData feeds in some jurisdictions (structured data export)
  • Bulk data agreements (negotiated with individual courts or administrative offices)

Beyond Tyler, the remaining courts use:

  • Journal Technologies (eCourt) — owned by Catalis, used in California, Arizona, others
  • Catalis — various smaller CMS products
  • Custom/legacy systems — some courts still run bespoke mainframe or client-server systems
  • ICMS (Integrated Case Management System) — used in some federal bankruptcy courts

Section VIIWhat RECAP Proved

RECAP (stands for "PACER" spelled backwards) is a browser extension and server infrastructure built by the Free Law Project, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. It solved a simple but profound problem: every time someone pays to download a federal court document from PACER, RECAP captures and stores a free copy. One download = free forever.

How It Works

  1. User installs the RECAP browser extension (Chrome or Firefox)
  2. When the user downloads a document from PACER (paying $0.10/page), RECAP uploads a copy to the Free Law Project's servers
  3. When any future user searches for that document, RECAP checks its archive first and serves the free copy
  4. All collected documents are available on CourtListener.com, the Free Law Project's public search engine

The Results

RECAP / CourtListener by the Numbers

  • 200M+ pages of federal court documents archived
  • Millions of federal dockets searchable for free
  • 500K+ oral argument audio recordings
  • 8M+ judicial opinions in the database
  • $0 cost to the public for access
  • Budget: ~$1.5M/year (nonprofit, donation-funded)

What RECAP Proved

  • Crowdsourced collection works. A few thousand active users captured millions of documents.
  • The marginal cost of access is near zero. Once a document is collected, serving it costs fractions of a penny.
  • The government did not shut it down. PACER documents are public records. Redistributing them is legal.
  • Courts benefited. Judges and clerks use CourtListener. Transparency improved.
  • It created a platform. CourtListener's data powers legal research, journalism, and academic study.

Why Nobody Has Done This for State Courts

RECAP works because PACER is a single, federal system. One architecture, one authentication method, one fee structure. State courts are fundamentally different:

Dimension Federal (PACER) State Courts
Systems 1 (PACER/CM/ECF) 50+ states × multiple vendors = hundreds of interfaces
Authentication Single PACER login Per-state, per-county; some require in-person registration
Data Format Standardized Wildly inconsistent. Some courts provide searchable PDFs; others provide scanned images.
Anti-Scraping Minimal (PACER wants revenue, not security) CAPTCHAs, rate limiting, IP blocking, terms of service restrictions
Legal Clarity Clear: public records, redistribution legal Murkier: some states restrict bulk access, some TOS prohibit scraping
Volume ~800K cases/year ~20M+ cases/year

The technical challenge is real but solvable. Tyler Odyssey's dominance (2,400 courts) actually simplifies the problem: build one robust Odyssey integration, and you cover roughly 60–70% of state court volume. Add Journal Technologies and the handful of other CMS vendors, and you are at 85%+.

The real reason nobody has done it is economic: building and maintaining 50-state court integrations requires sustained investment. The Free Law Project has limited resources and has focused on federal courts, where one integration covers the entire system. The commercial players (LexisNexis, Thomson Reuters) have no incentive to make state court data free — they profit from the scarcity.

Section VIIIThe Opportunity

The opportunity has two components, and they are more powerful in combination than either is alone.

Component 1: RECAP for State Courts

Build an open, free, searchable archive of state court records. Use a combination of:

  • Automated collection from public court portals (Tyler Odyssey, Journal Technologies, etc.)
  • Crowdsourced uploads from attorneys, legal aid organizations, and pro se litigants
  • Bulk data agreements with forward-thinking court administrators
  • FOIA/public records requests in states with favorable open records laws

Priority targeting: start with the highest-volume debt collection courts. In Texas, a handful of justice of the peace courts in Harris, Dallas, Bexar, and Tarrant counties handle the majority of debt collection filings. In Illinois, Cook County alone processes hundreds of thousands of civil cases annually.

Component 2: Automated Legal Defense

Once you have the filing, AI can do what currently costs $5,000:

  1. Identify the claim type (debt collection, eviction, foreclosure, etc.)
  2. Extract key facts (amount, creditor, account number, date of default)
  3. Check the statute of limitations based on jurisdiction and debt type
  4. Identify applicable affirmative defenses (standing, SOL, improper service, FDCPA violations)
  5. Generate a plain-English explanation of what was filed and what the defendant's options are
  6. Draft an answer with proper formatting, affirmative defenses, and verification demands
  7. Provide filing instructions specific to the court and jurisdiction

The Economics

Metric Automated System Legal Aid Attorney Private Attorney
Cost per case $0.16 $500–$1,000 (subsidized) $5,000–$11,500
Time to generate response 2–5 minutes 2–5 days 3–10 days
Capacity Unlimited (scales with compute) 50–100 cases/attorney/year 100–200 cases/attorney/year

The math is simple. If an automated system prevents even 10% of the ~3.2 million annual default judgments in debt collection cases, and the average default judgment is $5,000, the annual value to consumers is $1.6 billion. The cost to run the system: approximately $500,000/year.

Legal Considerations

This is not the unauthorized practice of law (UPL). The system does not provide legal advice — it provides:

  • Access to public records (constitutionally protected)
  • Plain-English explanation of those records (journalism / education)
  • Self-help templates (protected under NAACP v. Button, 371 U.S. 415 (1963), and state access-to-justice initiatives)
  • Filing instructions (publicly available procedural information)

Multiple states have explicitly authorized self-help legal technology. Utah's regulatory sandbox, Arizona's elimination of the UPL prohibition for licensed legal paraprofessionals, and California's AB-2958 (2024) expanding permissible nonlawyer legal services all signal regulatory openness.

Section XThe Gap

Review every company, nonprofit, and academic project in Section IX. Not one of them offers the following combination:

  1. Affordable, real-time access to state court records — not $200/month, not $15,000/year, but free or near-free
  2. AI-powered comprehension of what was filed and what it means in plain English
  3. Automated legal defense document generation for consumers facing collection lawsuits, evictions, and other high-volume civil matters
  4. Filing guidance specific to the jurisdiction, court, and case type

The Product That Does Not Exist

Imagine a person is served with a debt collection lawsuit. Today, they have three options: hire an attorney they cannot afford, try to navigate the system alone with no information, or do nothing and lose by default.

The product that does not exist would work like this:

  1. The person enters their case number (or it is detected automatically via public docket monitoring)
  2. The system retrieves the complaint and all filings from the court's public portal
  3. AI reads the filings and generates a plain-English summary: "You are being sued by Midland Credit Management for $9,350. They claim you owe this on a Chase credit card account. The statute of limitations in your state for written contracts is 5 years. The alleged default date is 2019, which means the statute may have expired."
  4. The system identifies applicable defenses and generates a draft answer
  5. The person receives step-by-step filing instructions for their specific court
  6. Total cost: $0 (subsidized) or $5–$25 (if revenue-generating)
  7. Time from service to filed answer: hours, not weeks

This product would convert a significant percentage of the 3.2 million annual default judgments into contested cases. Even a 10% conversion rate would represent 320,000 people per year who keep their wages, their bank accounts, and their credit scores intact.

Summary

The US legal information landscape is a system where public records are privatized, access is commoditized, and the people who need information most are the least able to obtain it. The constitutional right of access exists in theory. In practice, it is paywalled behind Tyler Technologies' infrastructure, Thomson Reuters' editorial layer, and LexisNexis' analytics platform.

The technology to change this costs approximately $200/month in infrastructure plus $0.16 per case in AI processing. The annual value of preventing even a fraction of default judgments is measured in billions. The legal framework (First Amendment, state open records laws, access-to-justice movements, regulatory sandboxes) increasingly supports this work.

What is missing is not technology, not legal authority, and not demand. What is missing is the system itself: a state-court RECAP with AI-powered defense generation that serves the 80% of civil defendants who currently lose by default because they cannot access the information they need to fight back.
