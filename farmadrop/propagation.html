⚠ OPEN QUESTION · WHICH NETWORK?

"The 12,000 churches" resolves to two very different networks — and they imply opposite go-to-market motions. Assemblies of God (12,651, 2024) has a semi-centralized district polity: a real top-down channel where seeding a few districts can cascade. Churches of Christ (11,776, 2022; 1,901 in Texas) are fully autonomous congregations: no headquarters, so growth is pure one-church-at-a-time peer contagion. Before tuning any diffusion parameter, Volkmar & Magnus must say which one they mean — it is the difference between selling to districts and selling to 12,000 independent front doors.

Section 1

The model — churches diffuse, users follow

A discrete monthly Bass diffusion spreads adoption across a bounded set of M churches: ΔN = (p + q·N/M)·(M − N) — an external term p (advertising, the founders' direct outreach) plus a peer-contagion term q (churches copying churches). Each adopting church converts to weekly users: users(t) = N(t) × attendance × 0.43 households/attendee × participation(t), and weekly gross = users × basket. Participation ramps 3% → 8% → 12% over the first year, then holds.

Assumptions baked into this model (1) Every church is the same median size (70) — reality is heavily skewed: ~50% of churchgoers sit in the largest ~10% of churches (Lifeway 2025), so which churches adopt matters as much as how many. (2) 0.43 households/attendee (~2.3 people/household). (3) A single q for the whole network — but an autonomous network (CoC) and a district network (AoG) do not share a contagion rate. (4) Participation holds flat after year 1 with no churn. Every one of these is optimistic; the chart below shows what they produce.

Precedents — the center dies, the network survives

Church-hosted food distribution has scaled to national size before. Every collapse happened at the central node — never in the church layer.

Regional and slow for ~10 years — then a 2005 $6.9M USDA warehouse loan unlocked the logistics backbone and it went vertical: ~5,000 host churches, half a million families a month. It did not die from the church network failing; it died from founder fraud at the center. The takeaway for Farmer Drop is twofold: (1) the hockey stick came from central logistics capital, not from more churches; (2) the fatal risk lives at the center, in governance and money integrity.

USDA Farmers-to-Families

~176M food boxes distributed through faith & nonprofit partners — then ended by policy, not by demand. Precedent that a subsidy-dependent center is a policy risk: when the funding stopped, the distribution stopped.

SHARE Food Network

~300 church host sites, ~12,000 families/month in the DC area. Eroded competitively as grocery discounting and online delivery caught up; survived by institutionalizing (becoming a nonprofit food hub) rather than staying a volunteer buying club.

    The honest headline Growth speed and survival are separate axes. The Bass sliders tune speed; but every precedent above died at the center — fraud (Angel Food), subsidy withdrawal (USDA F2F), or competitive erosion (SHARE) — and never in the church layer. Governance and funding integrity is an independent failure variable this model does not capture. A perfect q does not protect you from a compromised center.
