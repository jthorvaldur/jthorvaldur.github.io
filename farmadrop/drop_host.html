Section 1

What a HostDrop is

Not a warehouse, not a store — the pickup point. A member orders on the platform; the food is hauled from the farm to the nearest HostDrop; the member collects it inside a pickup window. Everything the network does upstream (routing, tokens, Drop Score) exists to fill these atoms. The map counts ~5 hosts today; ideally each is wrapped as a PMA.

What it needs to function

  • Cold + dry space — a fridge/freezer and shelf for the pickup window; the binding constraint for perishables.
  • A pickup window — a predictable few-hour slot members can rely on (the retail "open hours").
  • A coordinator — one human who receives the haul, checks it in, and hands baskets to members. The single point of local trust.
  • A location members already go to — church, farm, home, or small shop. Convenience is the whole product.
Assumption "Fridge + window + coordinator" is inferred from the map's description of hosts as aggregation/pickup nodes — the map never enumerates minimum host requirements. Confirm the real spec before recruiting.

Who can be one

Churches / parish halls Farms (host their own drop) Homes (a neighbor's garage fridge) Small shops / co-ops Community centers

The host is recruited & trained by an independent contractor who earns 3% of that host's drop volume for a fixed window (out of the platform's 10% take, §3.1). Churches carry a built-in fundraising angle — see §4.

If an Uber-style driver (anyone monetizing an idle truck) hauls it, that 5% goes to the driver instead. The contractor's 3% is carved out of the platform's take, not added on top.

Section 4

Church-as-host — the parish hall earns 5% of every basket

The median US congregation is ~70 weekly attendees ≈ ~30 households (NCS-IV / FACT). At an early 12% weekly participation that is roughly 3–4 baskets a week. The fundraiser framing writes itself: the church hosts the drop, and the parish hall earns 5% of every basket that passes through it — a recurring, effortless collection plate tied to groceries the congregation already buys.

Congregation math (live basket, $35 default)

The fundraiser framing

"Add your congregation. Every grocery basket a member picks up here returns 5% to the parish hall — no bake sale, no volunteer shift beyond the pickup window. Early it is a few dollars a week; at 12% participation across a 70-seat church it is a modest but recurring line, and it scales with the congregation."

Reality check At the default $35 basket the early number is small — about /week. The fundraising story is real but modest at median scale; it becomes material only with larger congregations (attendee-weighted mean ~360), higher baskets, or higher participation. Do not oversell the collection plate.

Cite: median weekly attendance ≈ 70 — U.S. Congregational Life Survey / Faith Communities Today (FACT) & National Congregations Study (NCS-IV). See propagation.html for the network-growth model built on this atom.

Section 5

Failure modes — the honest almanac

Community-hosted food distribution has a long, instructive graveyard. These are the ways the drop-host layer breaks, drawn from real precedents.

  • Host churn & volunteer burnout. The coordinator is unpaid attention, not salaried labor. SHARE Food Network and Bountiful Baskets both leaned on volunteer host-site coordinators; burnout and turnover were chronic — Bountiful Baskets wound down in 2018.
  • Cold-chain & food safety at volunteer sites. A church fridge is not a licensed commissary. Perishables held in uninspected volunteer space is a liability and a regulatory exposure the map does not price in.
  • Hosts as single points of local trust. One coordinator per neighborhood means one person's reliability gates the whole local network. If they quit, move, or mishandle a hand-off, that geography goes dark with no redundancy.
  • Cash-handling & reconciliation. Any cash at pickup, or credits owed to the host, invites shrinkage and disputes. The 5% host cut must be auto-settled by the ledger, never hand-counted — otherwise trust erodes fast.
  • The 5% + 5% + 10% + 3% stack vs the 80% promise. Host, haul, platform, and contractor all draw from the same basket. The map promises the farmer ~80% and a decentralized set of paid intermediaries — the arithmetic only closes if the take is ~10% (not 0.10%) and hops stay at one. Every added hop or a higher take breaks either the farmer promise or the member price. This is the load-bearing tension of the whole model.
