Section 1

The tracker contract — state in, state out

Every forecaster in the engine is a skater: a pure function fed one observation and its own previous state, returning a forecast and the next state. No history buffer, no refitting job — a lane's entire model is a handful of numbers that can be stored in a row and replayed deterministically. That is what makes 10,000 lanes as cheap as one.

Section 2

Forgetting — the EWMA baseline

The average flow of a lane is an exponentially weighted moving average: level += α · (y − level). One knob, α, sets the memory: weight on a week that is w weeks old decays like (1−α)w, so the "effective window" is ≈ 1/α weeks. Drag α and watch the trade: small α is smooth but late; large α is current but jumpy.

Section 3

Outliers — seen, not believed

Inject a few spike weeks (a festival, a glitch) into the same lane. The plain EWMA swallows the spike — the baseline jumps and takes ~1/α weeks to recover, mis-stating the lane the whole way down. The robust tracker clips the residual at k·b before believing it (huberization): the level barely moves, while the raw residual still fires the anomaly score. Turn the spike size up — the gap between the two lines is the cost of trusting outliers.

Section 4

Why Laplace, not Gaussian

Two choices hide inside every "average ± spread" statement: the loss (squared error → mean & variance; absolute error → median & mean-absolute-deviation) and the tail story. Squared errors make one 10× week matter 100×, so a Gaussian tracker's spread explodes on a single spike. The Laplace choice is the L1 version of the same machinery: scale b = EWMA of |residual|, linear in outliers — and its tail probability has a form you can do in your head.

The shapes · same scale, different tails

Gaussian Laplace — peakier AND fatter-tailed

Surprise = −log P(|resid| ≥ r) · in nats

Gaussian — quadratic: far things look impossible Laplace — LINEAR: surprise = r / b
Under Laplace, surprise IS the normalized residual: |y − level| ÷ b, in nats.
3 nats ⇒ e−3 ≈ a 1-in-20-weeks tail event for that lane's own noise. A Gaussian model calls the same week a 1-in-thousands event, cries wolf on every fat-tailed series, and then gets ignored. Heavy tails are what lane data actually has — Laplace prices them honestly. (skaters goes one step further: a Gaussian scale mixture — a Student-t built from components at 0.7/1/1.6/3/6 × σ — so the tail shape survives ensembling. That's our v1.)
Section 5

Thinking fast and slow — two speeds, blended by skill

One α can't be right on both sides of a regime change — a new HostDrop opens nearby and a lane's true level jumps. So run two trackers: fast (α=0.4, believes recent weeks) and slow (α=0.05, believes the long run), and blend them by inverse recent error. In calm times the slow tracker wins the weight and the blend is smooth; right after the break the fast tracker is simply more correct, takes the weight, and hands it back once the slow one catches up. Nobody decides "regime change" — the weights just follow the errors.

Section 7

Hot / cold — thresholds you can say out loud

Put it together: the robust tracker gives every lane a level and a personal noise scale b. A week is hot when its residual exceeds the surprise threshold in its OWN units — |y − level| / b ≥ s nats ⇒ a ≤ e−s tail week. Compare that with the fixed-ratio rule (hot if > 1.25 × baseline): on a jittery lane the ratio cries constantly; on a quiet lane it misses real events. Surprise adapts because b is the lane's own history.

Section 8

What's next — the rest of the skaters toolbox

Concept What it buys the engine
PIT / z calibration Is the model honest about its own uncertainty? Feed each realized week back through the predictive CDF — the results should be uniform. Systematic skew = fix the model, not the threshold. Anomaly flag at |z| > 4.
Periodicity detection An online EWMA-autocorrelation scan over candidate lags. We assumed the 52-week rhythm; real demand may carry biweekly pay-cycles or monthly patterns — detect them, don't assume them.
Gaussian scale mixtures Student-t tails built from Gaussian components (0.7/1/1.6/3/6 × σ) with online likelihood weights — heavier-than-Laplace tails that survive ensemble averaging.
Log-evidence ensembles with forgetting Upgrade our inverse-error blend: weight every candidate tracker by exponentially-discounted log-likelihood (forget ≈ 0.99) minus a complexity penalty. Regime shifts re-weight in weeks, automatically.
One-scorer benchmarking Every forecaster — including naive last-value — scores through the same logpdf + CRPS scorer after a burn-in. A predictor earns its complexity on the synthetic world before it touches real lanes.
