Section 3

Lanes — two views of the same flow

Each lane keeps an average flow (its learned baseline) and a real-time flow (this week). Two ways to judge them, shown side by side: the naive fixed ratio (hot if >1.25× baseline) and the engine's Laplace surprise — how many of the lane's own typical deviations this week is. A ratio that looks alarming on a jittery lane can be ordinary; the same ratio on a metronomic lane is an event. That distinction is exactly what the robust math buys — see it derived.

Lanebaseline (EWMA)robust level Laplace bthis week surprise (nats)ratio viewsurprise view

surprise = |this week − robust level| ÷ b. Under Laplace noise P(surprise ≥ s) = e−s: a lane at 3.0 nats is a ≈1-in-20-weeks event for that lane. Disagreements between the two views (tagged ≠) are the whole argument.

Section 5

Driver coordination — bundle the corridor

Lanes into the same HostDrop whose farmers sit near each other are bundle candidates: one driver, one run, two pickups. v0 flags them; v1 turns flags into routes priced against the 5% haul.

Section 6

Seasonality — the curves the engine recovered

The generator hides a true seasonal curve inside each category's demand; the engine fits annual + semi-annual harmonics to the noisy weekly totals and must recover the peak. Fitted curve in honey, true peak as the dashed line. This is the forward-scaling layer: baselines × these factors = what next month's lanes look like.
