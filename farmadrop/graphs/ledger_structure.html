§1

Why double-entry, why six

Every dollar in the fee model is a conservation law: a member's $100 basket must land, to the cent, across farmer, hops, take, margin, surcharges and tax — and every unwind must land back. Double-entry is the proof mechanism: a transaction that doesn't balance can't be written. Separate books exist because the money has structurally different lifecycles — cash settles on Stripe's clock, credits are a standing liability, farmer payables release per delivery, commissions accrue over months, tax lives on the filing calendar. Reconciling six books against each other (and against Stripe's balance_transactions, and later chain==event==DB==UI on the token rail) is what turns "we think the money is right" into arithmetic.

    Alternates — kept alive, both are re-tags

    H2 — clearing variant. Farmer and hop payables merge into one "everyone we owe" book; the sixth ledger is an Orders / Receivables clearing book (authorized-not-captured, undelivered orders). Supported by the test-app flow — "purchases with the Stripe card, no money withdrawn" is exactly a clearing-ledger state. Regroup: re-tag 2150 under the payables book, promote 1100.

    H3 — rail-split variant. The cash book splits fiat vs token-rail wallet (CPDR / Base), anticipating Phase 1 — per the compadres chain==event==DB==UI reconciliation doctrine. Regroup: split L1 on the rail dimension. Neither alternate changes a single posting rule.

    What "six reconcile" means in the partition view (C-3). A partition is one journal with tags — an individual book does not balance on its own. What it does give you: each book's net position is its implied due-to/due-from against the other books, and the six nets sum to zero — that identity is the cross-book reconciliation, and the simulator displays it live per book and checks Σ = 0. Whether Volkmar's six instead balance internally via explicit due-to/due-from accounts is exactly what RQ-1 asks.

    §4

    The unwind paths

    This is the page's reason to exist. Three rules make unwinding survivable: history is append-only (every unwind is a new compensating entry — nothing is edited); reverse at the original (tax rate, jurisdiction, take-% are stamped on the line at capture and every reversal computes from the stamp); and the legs are authoritative (the customer refund amount is the sum of per-leg reversals, matching per-line Stripe Tax reversal amounts — not a re-derived percentage).

    The Stripe Tax partial-reversal caveat — the named pain, verified.
    Stripe Tax reverses a recorded tax.Transaction with mode=full or mode=partial (per-line line_items[] with negative amount/amount_tax, or a flat_amount distributed proportionally across remaining line items). But reversals do not adjust taxable/nontaxable amounts in reports and carry no reversal reason — Stripe itself recommends fully reversing the transaction and re-creating a corrected one when accuracy matters. Working strategy: same-period, clean pro-rata → mode=partial, mirrored 1:1 in the ledger; cross-period or mixed-taxability → full-reverse-and-recreate, with the ledger booking the amended-return posture. Cap: 30 partial reversals per sale; over-reversal errors out. What the .7 backend does today is RQ-3.
    §5

    Extensibility — accounts stay stable, dimensions absorb variety

    Regional tomatoes, a neighbor's hot sauce, farmer #10,000, the Phase-1 transfer coin: none of these are new accounts. New accounts appear only when a genuinely new kind of money appears. Everything else rides one of three dimension axes.

    Axis 1 — product tax code on order lines
    Tomatoes are grocery-exempt in 37 states; shelf-stable hot sauce usually rides the exemption but candy/cottage-food carve-outs can flip it. Taxability is a line-item property resolved against the pickup jurisdiction at capture, and the resolved rate is stamped on the line.
    Encoding this as accounts would need product × jurisdiction combinatorics. As a dimension, a new product or state is a data row — and the partial-refund machinery already keys off the line stamp, so it just works.
    Axis 2 — party subaccount control children
    New farmer → 2100.<farmer_id>; new hop/driver → 2150.<hop_id>; new contractor accrues under 2200. Control accounts and every posting rule stay fixed.
    Subaccounts give real-time per-entity balances (the Airbnb subledger pattern); party #10,000 posts to the same journal as party #1.
    Axis 3 — rail 10xx cash family
    Phase-1 transfer coin / Phase-2 Honey Coin = a new 10xx cash account (1050 Rail Wallet). The allocation and refund journals are byte-identical — only the money account swaps. This is H3, achieved as a dimension instead of a fork.
    ⚠ The one thing the rail axis does NOT absorb: tax on non-card rails — Stripe Tax is card-coupled. See hard case #6 and RQ-12.
    §6

    Time — the four clocks

    Refunds are hard because four independent clocks run over the same journal. Each clock gates different events and imposes its own invariant.

    The bitemporal verdict — phased, not all-or-nothing

    The trigger cases are genuinely bitemporal, not merely append-only: a refund-after-filing asserts a new truth about a past period while preserving what was filed as-known then (valid time ≠ transaction time — the textbook case); a dispute lost on day 80 is effective at the charge's economic date but known two months later; a retroactively misclassified product (hot sauce reclassified taxable) is a corrected-timeline look-back; and "the balance as the .7 backend believed it on date X" is, definitionally, a transaction-time slice.

    Phase 0: carry both timestamps on every journal row; do not build the as-of query engine yet. Two columns, near-zero cost, fully forward-compatible. Defer the full AUDIT/as-of layer until the first amended return forces it — that event is the concrete forcing function. Omitting the timestamps now is the only expensive mistake.

    journal_row: id, event_id (idempotency key), account, dr|cr, amount_cents,
             effective_at,    -- valid time: the economic / pickup date
             posted_at,       -- transaction time: system insert, immutable
             corrects_row_id  -- nullable; links a contra to what it reverses

    ⚠ Assumption: the .7 backend stores at least posted_at immutably. Implication if wrong: no past-as-believed balance is reconstructable, and RQ-2 / RQ-4 become blocking.
