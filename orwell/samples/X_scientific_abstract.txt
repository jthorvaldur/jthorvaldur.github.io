Embedding Dimensionality and Information Bottleneck in Legal Language Models

We measure the intrinsic dimensionality of contract-language embeddings under three models: BGE-large-en-v1.5, OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, and a domain-adapted SPLADE++ checkpoint trained on 1.2M legal clauses. Across a corpus of 47,000 contracts spanning 12 sub-domains, we find that 93% of variance in pairwise cosine similarity is captured by the first 38 principal components, despite the nominal embedding dimensions of 1024, 3072, and 30000 respectively.

This "dimensionality illusion" has practical consequences for retrieval pipelines. We show that pruning to the top 64 components reduces storage by 16x with retrieval@10 dropping only 2.1 percentage points. The effect is more pronounced in template-heavy clauses (force majeure, indemnification) and weaker in fact-specific clauses (warranties, schedules).

We introduce the Dictionary Collapse Ratio (DCR) — the ratio of effective rank to embedded rank — as a domain-agnostic readout of how much of an embedding's apparent capacity is used. For legal contracts, mean DCR is 0.041. For Wikipedia abstracts, mean DCR is 0.187. The four-fold gap quantifies how much legal writing reuses fixed phrasal templates.

Code, evaluation harness, and the pruned indices are released at github.com/jthorvaldur/embedding-dim.
