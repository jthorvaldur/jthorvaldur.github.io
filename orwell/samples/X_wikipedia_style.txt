Geodesic Distance on Statistical Manifolds

In information geometry, a statistical manifold is a Riemannian manifold whose points are probability distributions and whose metric is the Fisher information matrix. The geodesic distance between two distributions, called the Fisher-Rao distance, generalizes the notion of distance between points in Euclidean space to distributions.

For two discrete distributions P and Q on a finite alphabet, the Fisher-Rao distance is given by 2 times the arc cosine of the Bhattacharyya coefficient. The Bhattacharyya coefficient is the sum over outcomes of the geometric mean of the two probabilities. The resulting distance is bounded between zero and pi, with zero indicating identical distributions and pi indicating distributions with disjoint support.

The Fisher-Rao metric is invariant under reparameterization, which makes it the natural choice for comparing distributions across different coordinate systems. It also coincides with the spherical metric on the simplex of probability distributions, embedded via the square-root map.

Applications include hypothesis testing, where the Fisher-Rao distance bounds the type-1 and type-2 error rates of optimal detectors, and information retrieval, where it provides a principled alternative to Euclidean distance on bag-of-words representations.
